## Problem Statement

Musicians who run Ableton Link alongside hardware (loopers like the Boss RC-505,
Eurorack, drum machines) can't get rock-solid tempo sync. Neither Ableton nor the
hardware behaves well as a *follower*, and neither is a great *master*. The fix the
market knows is "buy a solid external clock" — but the options are expensive,
closed, and none is Link-native and affordable at the same time. There is a
working, framework-portable clock engine already built here (KitchenSync /
X32Link, pure-C per ADR-0003) that can be that master, on a cheap chip.

## Demand Evidence

- **Dan Kushner (named customer), verbatim:** "I have a clock issue. I want to sync
  my RC 505 and Ableton. Neither does well when not the master. What I've heard is a
  solid clock like the erm can get rid of these issues, but I don't want to spend
  $400 on speculation." Would pay **~$100** for a Link→MIDI device.
- **Juanito Moore / Modular for the Masses (named customer + partner):** interested;
  would co-design a custom PCB and sell through his channel. Ships 15+ open-source
  Eurorack modules in C/C++ (EasyEi8ht ⭐37, HexKlox ⭐13). Real hardware collaborator,
  not just a fan.
- **Distribution:** both are influential in the synth world and **have existing synth
  buyer lists**; both will be at **KnobCon** (a dated launch venue with the exact
  audience).
- **Honest gap:** zero paying customers today. This is pre-product. Demand is
  *validated in words + one price point*, not yet in money. Category demand is
  proven (E-RM Multiclock, CLOCKstep, the discontinued-but-beloved Missing Link).

## Status Quo

What a Link-using musician does today to sync hardware:
- **E-RM Multiclock** (~$400, closed) — the "gold standard" Dan won't gamble on.
- **CLOCKstep:MULTI** ($249, closed) — precise, but **no Ableton Link at all**
  (DIN/USB/audio only).
- **Missing Link** (Circuit Happy) — did exactly Link→MIDI/analog, had a cult
  following, **discontinued for business reasons, not lack of demand** — leaving a
  hole in the market.
- **Drag a laptop** and let it be the clock master (USB-MIDI jitter, babysitting).
- Dan specifically: fights the master/follower battle between his RC-505 and Ableton
  and loses.

## Target User & Narrowest Wedge

**The human:** Dan — desktop/looper musician, RC-505 + Ableton, budget-conscious
("won't spend $400 on speculation"), wants a rock-solid external master both follow.
**Juanito's audience** is the second beachhead: budget DIY/open modular builders
("for the masses," tuna-can modular, open-source everything).

**The wedge — one sentence:** *the little box that makes your Ableton and your
hardware lock to a rock-solid master — Link-native, no laptop, for way under the
E-RM's $400, and you can try it before you trust it.*

**Key mechanism (the composability unlock):** a tiny ESP32 brick takes Ableton Link
in over WiFi and puts out clock at whatever rate each destination wants —
**24 PPQN** (pulses per quarter-note = MIDI clock) out the MIDI DIN to gear like the
RC-505, and **4 PPB** (pulses per beat = 16th-note triggers) into modular clock
inputs, which is exactly what Juanito's HexKlox external-trigger input expects. The
existing `clock_output` module already does arbitrary per-output division, so this
is configuration, not new firmware (confirmed via codebase index: `clock_output`
is a shared, host-tested pure-C module reused across three targets — X32Link,
X32MidiClock, KitchenSync). The brick
doesn't compete with HexKlox or any clock module — it makes all of them Link-native.
It removes the laptop *as the clock master/babysitter*, not the laptop (Ableton
still runs and joins as a Link peer).

**Network:** the brick hosts its own WiFi (soft-AP) by default so "no router, no
laptop babysitting" is literally true on any stage; optionally joins an existing
network. Provisioning is a one-time config (the web UI already exists).

**The hard part (name it, don't hide it):** turning jittery Link-over-WiFi into a
stable *local* clock is the actual engineering risk, not the MIDI/trigger output.
The device must recover clean timing from the network (the ghost-xform / phase-lock
work already started, plus disabling WiFi power-save) and the output jitter has to
be measurably low. This is where "rock-solid" is earned — see Success Criteria.

## Constraints

- Solo builder + Juanito as a capable co-design partner (owns the PCB/channel/audience).
- Juanito's channel ethos is **cheap + DIY + open-source** — a $180 sealed premium
  module does NOT fit it; kits / bare PCBs / accessible assembled units do.
- Must undercut E-RM ($400) and CLOCKstep ($249) decisively.
- **KnobCon** is the launch deadline (dated event; forces shipping over polishing).
- The clock engine already exists and is framework-portable (ADR-0003) — it runs on
  the S3 and the P4 today; porting to a cheap chip is a small job, not a rewrite.
- The $40 ESP32-P4-NANO is the wrong chip for a $100 product; the wedge needs a
  $3–5 ESP32-S3 or C3.
- **COGS (rough, assembled brick):** ESP32-S3 module ~$3–5 + MIDI DIN jack + opto/
  driver ~$2 + enclosure ~$5–12 + USB PSU/cable ~$2–3 → **~$12–22 BOM**. At $79–99
  retail that leaves real margin *and* room for a Juanito rev-share. A kit / bare
  PCB is cheaper still for his DIY audience. (Refine before committing.)
- **Regulatory:** build on a **pre-certified ESP32 module** (FCC/CE at the module
  level) so assembled units are sellable; bare PCB / kits sidestep it entirely.

## Premises

1. The wedge is "rock-solid, Link-native, cheap, de-risked clock master," **not** a
   feature-maximized do-everything box. The "everything and the kitchen sync" vision
   is the dream; the wedge is the opposite. — agree/disagree?
2. **Ship the companion brick first** (Link → MIDI 24-PPQN + 4-PPB trigger), because
   it's the smallest thing, has a paying customer (Dan), needs zero change to
   HexKlox, and composes with the whole modular-clock ecosystem. — agree/disagree?
3. **Shared core on a cheap chip** (ESP32-S3 or C3), not the P4. The P4/KitchenSync
   flagship is the someday-expansion, not the wedge. — agree/disagree?
4. **Open-core** (open the firmware + hardware, sell assembled units + kits) is the
   native language of Juanito's channel and how boutique modular actually earns
   trust (Music Thing, Mutable). — agree/disagree?
5. **Validate with the prototype you already have** before spending a dollar on a
   custom PCB: put a working unit on Dan's RC-505 + Ableton this week. — agree/disagree?

## Approaches Considered

### Approach A: Companion brick first, HexKlox-Link second (RECOMMENDED)
Ship a tiny ESP32-S3/C3 "Link brick": Link in → 24-PPQN MIDI out (RC-505) + 4-PPB
trigger out (modular / HexKlox clock-in), open-core, ~$79–99 assembled + a kit.
Validate with the existing prototype on Dan's rig now. Then the deeper Juanito
collaboration: **HexKlox Link Edition** — rehost his Arduino clock-gen onto an ESP32
and fold in the Link engine, one integrated module, launched at KnobCon.
- Effort: **S** (brick) then **M–L** (HexKlox v2). Risk: **Low** (brick), Med (v2).
- Reuses: the entire pure-C engine (`beat_source`, `clock_output`, `transport`,
  `usb_midi_pack`, `link_*`), unchanged, on a cheaper chip.

### Approach B: Eurorack module first
Lead with a from-scratch KitchenSync Eurorack module through Juanito's channel/margin.
- Effort: **L**. Risk: **Med–High** — more hardware (±12V, CV, panel, calibration)
  before any validated sale, and it competes with / duplicates HexKlox rather than
  composing with it.

### Approach C: P4 flagship first
Finish KitchenSync-on-P4 as the $249 do-everything box, head-to-head with CLOCKstep.
- Effort: **XL**. Risk: **High** — unknown challenger vs. an established product, wrong
  price/BOM for the customer you actually have, no wedge.

## Recommended Approach

**A.** It's the smallest shippable thing, it has the one paying customer, it needs no
change to HexKlox, and it turns Juanito's flagship (and every modular clock) into a
Link follower instead of a competitor. It validates the "engine on a cheap chip"
port with near-zero risk, funds and audience-builds the deeper HexKlox-Link
collaboration, and lands at KnobCon through two trusted voices with existing buyers.
Win the wedge, expand from strength.

**The real frame: you're not shipping a product, you're shipping the first device
off a platform.** `link-devices` is a **monorepo device family** — X32Link (the
shipped S3 mixer-FX/Link/MIDI box), X32MidiClock, KitchenSync, the emulator, and
in-flight analog-sync (LNK-028) work — all riding **one shared pure-C clock/sync
engine** that already runs across three targets. So the wedge isn't "build a
product," it's "sell the first device off a platform you already have." The **moat**
is exactly that: a competitor (or a discontinued Missing Link) has to rebuild the
engine; you spin out the next device — Link brick, HexKlox-Link, Eurorack analog
module, mixer-FX box — as another package off the same host-tested core, cheaply.
That matches how the channel actually buys: Juanito sells a *catalog*, not a module;
Dan's and his audiences buy device families. The Link brick is the first to market;
the family is the business.

## Open Questions

- **Open vs. closed:** evidence strongly favors open-core for this channel; needs your
  explicit call (affects licensing + how you split with Juanito).
- **Chip:** lean **ESP32-S3** as the shared core — the real reason isn't just GPIO,
  it's that the C3 is single-core and has no native USB-MIDI (USB-Serial/JTAG only),
  so WiFi + Link + tight timing recovery on one RISC-V core is risky headroom. S3
  gives a second core for timing and native USB-MIDI for the RC-505's USB port. (C3
  stays viable only for a DIN-only, MIDI-clock-out kit.)
- **Partnership terms with Juanito:** co-design/rev-share vs. paid design vs. favor —
  formalize before the custom PCB, not after.
- **Competitor facts to verify before this drives spend:** Dan quoted the E-RM at
  "$400" — but the E-RM *Multiclock* street price is materially higher (~$600+; the
  ~$150 Midiclock+ is the cheap one), so confirm which unit Dan means and don't let
  the "undercut $400" framing rest on his memory. (CLOCKstep's lack of Ableton Link
  was verified from the JMK product page this session.)
- **KnobCon date** and what's demo-able by then.

## Success Criteria

- **Timing is measurably tight:** output clock jitter under a target (e.g. **< 1 ms**
  at the DIN/trigger) and **no static beat offset** — this is the "rock-solid" claim
  made falsifiable. Prove it on the bench before Dan sees it.
- Dan doesn't want to give the loaner back / pre-orders a unit.
- A shared-core prototype on a cheap chip locks RC-505 + Ableton and feeds a modular
  clock-in (4 PPB).
- Juanito commits (in verbs) to co-design and sell.
- First units sold at KnobCon; open-source repo has external interest.

## The Assignment

**Get your existing working prototype onto Dan's RC-505 + Ableton — but close the
timing bugs first.** His whole objection is "speculation," so kill it with the
hardware you already have, on his actual rig, before you spend a dollar on a custom
PCB. If he doesn't want to give it back, you've validated the wedge for $0 on the
"wrong" expensive board — worth more than any spec sheet.

**One gate, non-negotiable:** the repo still has open phase/offset timing issues
(re-origin garbage, a static ~2-beat offset noted in recent commits). **Do not loan
a unit that's beats-off** — that's a *negative* validation event you can't take back
with a customer. Either (a) close the phase/offset bug first, or (b) explicitly scope
Dan's test to **tempo-lock only** ("does it hold BPM rock-solid?") and tell him up
front it's not yet phase-accurate. A clean tempo-lock demo is still a real yes; a
2-beats-off demo is a real no.
