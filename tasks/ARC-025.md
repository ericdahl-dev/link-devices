## The floor we have hit

After P4-033 (blocking log in the RT loop), P4-034 (single in-flight USB transfer) and ESP-018
(writer below lwIP), the clock is clean: **0 stalls, 0 bursts, 0 dropped pulses** under load. The
bugs are gone.

What is left is not a bug. It is the architecture:

| Device | Measured jitter (stdev) |
|---|---|
| KitchenSync (P4) | **331 µs** |
| KitchenSync Touch / X32Link (S3) | **~500 µs** |

All three firmwares generate MIDI clock from a **1 ms FreeRTOS task** (`vTaskDelayUntil`, tick
rate 1000 Hz). A pulse can only be emitted on a tick boundary. **A 1 ms quantisation grid cannot
produce sub-tick placement**, so ~300-500 µs of jitter is not something we are doing wrong -- it
is the direct, arithmetic consequence of the mechanism.

Every remaining microsecond is now paid for by the scheduler, and no amount of priority tuning
gets under it. Priority 19 fixed *starvation*. It cannot fix *granularity*.

## Why the two devices differ (and why it is not a P4 win)

The P4's 331 µs is better than the S3's ~500 µs **because its WiFi lives on a separate C6
co-processor over SDIO** -- its CPU never runs the network stack at all. The S3 runs WiFi on-die
and has to out-rank it.

This matters for scoping: the gap between them is a *contention* story, and it is already
addressed. The **floor underneath both** is the tick, and that is what this ticket is about.
Chasing the 331/500 difference is chasing the wrong number.

(It is also the warning already recorded on P4-031: putting wired Ethernet on the P4 would move
lwIP back onto the P4's own CPU and hand it the ESP-018 bug it has been structurally immune to.)

## Solution: emit from a hardware timer

Move the 24-PPQN pulse emission off the RTOS tick and onto a **hardware timer** (`esp_timer` /
GPTimer), which fires at a microsecond-resolution deadline independent of the scheduler grid.

The design must not be a rewrite:

- **The pure engines do not change.** `clock_ticker`, `midi_clock_out`, `transport_launch` are
  host-tested and correct (ADR-0003). They already answer *"how many pulses are due at beat X."*
  This ticket changes **when we ask**, not what answers.
- The RT task's job becomes *scheduling the next deadline* rather than *polling a 1 ms grid*.
- **DIN is a UART write** -- cheap, and already the fast path. **USB-MIDI is not**: the P4's host
  stack has an in-flight-transfer constraint (P4-034) and cannot be driven from an ISR. Expect
  DIN and USB to need different emission paths, and expect the DIN win to be the real one.

## Do this as measurement first, code second

This project's entire method is *measure, don't guess*, and this is the ticket most likely to
punish a guess. Before writing the timer path:

- [ ] Establish the current baseline on the analyzer, at a known BPM, under load. (The rig exists:
      P4 DIN mirror on GPIO20, downbeat strobe on GPIO21; MIDI jack **pin 5 = data, pin 2 =
      ground** -- pin 4 is +5 V and reads flat high, which has already cost one capture.)
- [ ] Confirm the hypothesis directly: **is the jitter distribution quantised to ~1 ms?** If the
      pulses are not landing on tick boundaries, the premise is wrong and this ticket should be
      closed rather than built. Do not skip this step. The ESP-018 ticket shipped with a **wrong
      premise** about core assignment and only measurement caught it.
- [ ] Then prototype on **one** device, one output, DIN only. Measure. Only generalise if the
      number actually moves.

## The question nobody has asked yet

**Does it matter?** 331 µs is already well inside what most gear resolves, and the DIN↔USB skew
measured this cycle (**170 µs**) came in *below the 331 µs jitter floor* -- which is precisely why
the skew-compensation feature was **killed rather than built**. That was a good outcome: a
measurement deleted a feature.

The same discipline applies here. If a hardware timer buys 331 µs → 20 µs and no listener, no
sequencer, and no analyzer-visible artefact changes, then this is an engineering vanity metric and
should be closed as such.

**But we do not know**, and the honest reason to build it is that it is the last structural
limit left in the clock path. Measure the baseline, decide with the number in hand.

## Out of scope

- Changing the pure timing engines. They are right.
- Raising `CONFIG_FREERTOS_HZ`. Bumping to 1 kHz→ higher trades jitter for scheduler overhead
  across the whole system and is a worse version of the same idea.
